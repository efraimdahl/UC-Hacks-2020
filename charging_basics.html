How does EV-Charging work?

The world of EV-charging can be intimidating. Here is a quick introduction to make sense of the maze of different plugs, chargers, and technologies out there.

Vehicle Types

___________

HYBRID

Designed to improve efficiency, Hybrid vehicles use an internal combustion engine both to load a battery and to maintain high speed, while an electric motor is used to power the vehicle at lower velocity.

___________

PLUG-IN HYBRID

Plugin Hybrids work like convential hybrids but have the ability to charge the battery for the electric motor directly. They usually have the option for purely electric drive and the combustion engine jumps in if battery power is low.

___________

BATTERY ELECTRIC

Battery Electrics or just EVs drive purely electric and they have no combustion engine. Besides being the most environmentally friendly of the vehicle options, they also have on average a lower cost of maintenance, since purely electric drive systemes are simpler and have less moving parts than vehicles with internal combustion engines.

Charging Levels

There are three charging levels that range from a slow trickle to ultra fast, and most EVs can charge at all three.

Level 1

Slow and steady

Level one charging occurs when you plug your vehicle into a standart domestic 120V (3 pin) socket.

Charging speed: Up to 3kw, about 4 miles per hour of charging.

Most people use Level one charging to charge their vehicles overnight at home. This typically gives them enough range to get through the day.

Level 2

Standard Commercial Charging

Level two chargers (such as EV Box-chargers) connect to a 240 V output (similar to one you would connect an higher power AC unit or dryer to)

Charging speed: Up to 7kw, about 25 miles per hour of charging.

Most chargers you see around town for public use are Level two chargers and all EVs can plug into them.

Level 3

DC Fast Charging

Level three charging (supercharging) uses very high voltage (480V) to charge a vehicle. Such chargers are very expensive and rap up high electricity bills.

Charging speed: Up to 24 kw, up to 200 miles per hour of charging.

These larger, more expensive chargers are slowly popping up around the country and will soon make it much easier for to take longer road trips in an EV as they show up along highway corridors.

Connected Charging Infrastructure

Advanced Capabilities Through Networked Chargers

Some chargers work just like your phone charger, plug and go. But modern chargers (such as the EVBox chargers) are capable of much more. Network controlled chargers allow the user to remotly control and schedule charging sessions, take advantage of fluctuating energy prices, and a specific facilities electric capacity, handle payments and much more.

OCPP

An Open-Source Standard For EVSE Infrastructure

Open charge point protocoll (OCPP) is an industry protocoll for electric vehicle chargers, that regulates the software and hardware interaction. That means any OCPP charger is compatible with any OCPP Network provider, to allow maximum use flexibility. EVBox chargers and many others use the OCPP standart.

The J1772 (J-Plug) is the most commonn EV plug. All vehicles in the US and Canada are compatible with this type of plug.

ChaDeMo

....................

The CHAdeMO Plug is especially popular on the Asian market, with some installed in the US. Unlike the SAE combo it is compatible with Tesla’s vehicles. Not compatible for all EV’s

CCS (SAE Combo Plug)

....................

The SAE Combo CCS plug combines the slow J1772 AC Plug with a fast DC plug allowing for fast charging. Not compatible for all EV’s.

Tesla

....................

Tesla has strategically placed “superchargers” along popular routes in the US that are only compatible with Tesla vehicles, using this particular plug.
